About Me

Dron Lakhanpal

My name is Dron Lakhanpal, and I am an Early Childhood Education student at Humber Polytechnic, currently completing my third field placement at Kenollie Public School in Mississauga. I am passionate about creating meaningful, responsive learning environments that honour the individuality of every child. Throughout my program, I have had the privilege of working across multiple placement settings, and each experience has strengthened my commitment to building authentic, relationship-centred practice grounded in the principles of How Does Learning Happen? and the ELECT framework.

I believe that every child arrives with extraordinary potential — like a seed carrying within it the promise of growth. As an aspiring educator, my role is to cultivate the conditions that allow each child to flourish: offering consistent support, nurturing curiosity, celebrating families as essential partners, and ensuring that every child feels genuinely seen, safe, and celebrated. I am honoured to be on this journey and look forward to contributing with intention and care to the field of early childhood education.

Land Acknowledgement

Acknowledging the Land

I acknowledge that Humber Polytechnic and the communities in which I have completed my field placements are situated on the Ancestral and Treaty Lands of the Haudenosaunee, the Anishinaabe, and the Huron-Wendat peoples. This land is covered by the Dish With One Spoon Wampum Belt Covenant — a treaty between the Haudenosaunee Confederacy and the Anishinaabe peoples to share and care for the resources of the Great Lakes region. I am grateful to live, learn, and practice on this land, and I am committed to deepening my understanding of Indigenous ways of knowing and incorporating them respectfully into my work as an early childhood educator.

My Philosophy

Teaching & Learning as a Garden

In our ECE program at Humber Polytechnic, one of our most meaningful tasks was to develop a personal philosophy of teaching and learning — one that reflects our deepest beliefs about children, families, educators, and the learning process. After rich exploration across my placements and coursework, a powerful metaphor emerged for me: I see teaching and learning as a garden. Below is my philosophy vision board and the personal belief statements that give it shape and meaning.

Vision Board
Visual Philosophy

My Teaching Philosophy

I Believe
Core Beliefs

Six Belief Statements

🌱

Children

As the Seed

Children are like seeds — each one singular and extraordinary, carrying within them immense potential waiting to be awakened. With the right conditions, nurturing relationships, and intentional support, every child has the capacity to grow, bloom, and offer something irreplaceable to the world. I believe deeply in the competence and curiosity of every child I encounter.

🌿

Family

As the Roots

Families are the roots — the foundational source of love, identity, and belonging from which all of a child's growth flows. I believe in honouring families as the true experts on their children, and in building partnerships grounded in mutual respect, open communication, and shared purpose. When families feel welcomed and valued, children thrive.

🧑‍🌾

Educator

As the Gardener

Educators are the gardeners — observant, intentional, and deeply caring. We do not control how a child grows, but we thoughtfully prepare the environment, offer the nourishment of encouragement and challenge, and attend carefully to each child's unique rhythm and pace. I am committed to being a responsive, reflective educator who sees and celebrates every child.

🌍

Community

As the Soil

The community is the rich soil in which children's learning is embedded. Cultural context, community connections, shared languages, and diverse experiences give depth and texture to children's development. I believe in drawing on the full richness of our communities — including Indigenous ways of knowing — to create learning environments that feel welcoming and meaningful for every family.

☀️

Environment

As Sunlight & Water

The environment — both physical and emotional — acts as sunlight and water, providing the essential conditions that either open possibilities or constrain them. I am committed to designing thoughtful indoor and outdoor learning spaces that invite curiosity, spark wonder, and ensure that every child can fully participate and feel a deep sense of belonging.

📚

Curriculum

As the Seasons

Curriculum, like the seasons, provides meaningful rhythm, structure, and progression to children's learning journeys. I see curriculum not as a fixed script but as a living, emergent process — one that honours children's interests, responds to their strengths and needs, and grows in partnership with families and communities. Learning happens best when it is joyful, purposeful, and deeply connected to real life.

Kenollie Public School — February 2026

Pedagogical Documentation

During my placement at Kenollie Public School in February 2026, I participated in a group pedagogical documentation assignment with four colleagues. We observed children during a cup stacking pyramid challenge in the indoor auditorium and documented their learning through multiple perspectives. Each team member contributed a unique interpretation — capturing how the children demonstrated cognitive problem-solving, persistence, and social co-operation as they repeatedly rebuilt their structures and negotiated strategies with one another. Engaging in this collaborative documentation process deepened my understanding of how multiple lenses enrich our understanding of children's learning, and it reinforced my belief that observation is most powerful when it is shared and discussed. I have included this documentation in my portfolio because it represents my ability to contribute meaningfully to a professional team and to see children as competent, capable learners (Ontario Ministry of Education, 2014a, p. 6).

Learning Story: "Little Organizer"

Learning Story
"Little Organizer"  ·  March 18, 2025

I wrote this learning story after observing Grace, a two-year-old, during indoor free play on March 18, 2025. Grace spent an extended period gathering her toys one by one and carefully placing them into a blue basket — examining each one, holding two at once to compare, and making deliberate choices with focused intention. Writing this story in a strengths-based voice — speaking directly to Grace about what I witnessed — was a meaningful shift in how I understood documentation. A learning story is not just a record; it is a celebration. It makes a child's competence visible and communicates to families that their child is truly seen. I chose to include this story in my portfolio because it represents a milestone in my development as a reflective early childhood educator, and because it reflects my commitment to seeing every child as capable, curious, and rich in potential (Ontario Ministry of Education, 2014a).

Outdoor Play

Space Mission Jump Challenge — a movement-based outdoor experience grounded in children's natural curiosity and physical strength.

Kenollie Public School  ·  March 2026

This outdoor plan grew out of my observation of three children aged six and seven during an indoor soccer game on March 4, 2026. I noticed they were deeply motivated by movement, challenge, and the excitement of competing — adjusting their kicks, experimenting with force, and laughing with each attempt. In response, I designed the Space Mission Jump Challenge: a space-themed outdoor activity where children jumped between chalk-drawn planets while holding a "spaceship" ball. The imaginative theme gave the children a purpose beyond just jumping — they were astronauts on a mission. I chose to include this plan because it demonstrates how I moved from a careful observation to an intentional, inclusive planned experience that honoured children's physical strengths while building balance, coordination, and engagement through play (Ontario Ministry of Education, 2014a, p. 35).

St. Simon Stock Elementary  ·  November 2025

This family-focused plan came from my observation of morning drop-off at St. Simon Stock Elementary School on November 19, 2025. I noticed how families and the educator connected briefly but meaningfully at the gate — sharing information, exchanging warm greetings, and checking the children's files. I recognized that families already had a daily system they used and trusted. My planned response used that same system: a "Leaf Art at Home" activity sheet placed inside each child's file for families to take home and explore together. I chose this plan for my portfolio because it reflects my understanding that family engagement does not require a formal event — it requires meeting families where they are. This experience showed me that a small, accessible gesture delivered through a familiar channel can deepen the home-school connection in a genuine, respectful way (Ontario Ministry of Education, 2014a, p. 3).

Artifacts

Three meaningful artifacts representing significant learning experiences throughout my ECE studies.

Below are three meaningful artifacts from my ECE studies at Humber Polytechnic. Each one represents a significant learning experience that has shaped my professional identity and strengthened my commitment to becoming a skilled, thoughtful early childhood educator.

Artifact 1
Fine Motor Development

Fine Motor Sorting Activity

This artifact represents a fine motor activity designed to support children's developing coordination and control. Children used tweezers to pick up and sort pom-poms, which required focus, precision, and patience. Through this experience, I observed how children approached challenges differently — some experimented with grip techniques, while others took their time to build confidence. My role was to create an environment that encouraged independence while providing support when needed. This activity reflects my belief that learning happens through hands-on exploration and meaningful engagement. By offering open-ended materials with intentional tools, I was able to support both physical development and self-regulation in a way that was engaging and developmentally appropriate.

Fine Motor & Persistence

Lacing and Coordination Activity

This artifact highlights a lacing activity that supported children's fine motor development, sequencing skills, and persistence. As children threaded strings through pre-designed shapes, they demonstrated concentration and problem-solving as they figured out how to align the string with each hole. I observed that some children repeated patterns, while others explored their own approaches, showing individuality in learning. My role was to model the process, encourage effort, and allow space for children to work independently. This experience connects to my philosophy of supporting learning through play-based, hands-on opportunities that promote confidence and skill development. It also reflects my commitment to fostering patience and resilience as children engage in meaningful tasks.

Foundational Frameworks

Resources

Three key frameworks that have guided my professional practice and shaped my understanding of children's learning.

Presented below are the three foundational resources I regularly draw upon to ground my practice, strengthen my observation and planning, and ensure that my work with children and families is evidence-based, responsive, and aligned with the highest standards of early childhood education in Ontario.

Figure 1. How Does Learning Happen? Ontario's Pedagogy for the Early Years [Cover]. Ontario Ministry of Education (2014).

How Does Learning Happen? Ontario's Pedagogy for the Early Years

I chose How Does Learning Happen? because it focuses on the importance of relationships, belonging, and engagement in early childhood education. This resource helped me understand that learning is not just about outcomes, but about how children feel and experience their environment. In my practice, I used this framework to create a supportive and inclusive learning space where children felt comfortable exploring and expressing themselves.

For example, during my activities, I focused on observing children's interests and building on them rather than directing their learning. This approach helped me create more meaningful and responsive experiences. How Does Learning Happen? has influenced my teaching by encouraging me to prioritize relationships and emotional well-being as the foundation for all learning (Ontario Ministry of Education, 2014a).

Figure 2. Early Learning for Every Child Today (ELECT) [Cover]. Ontario Ministry of Education (2007).

Early Learning for Every Child Today (ELECT)

I chose the ELECT framework because it provides a clear understanding of child development and how learning occurs through play. This resource helped me connect children's behaviours with developmental domains such as physical, cognitive, and social-emotional growth. In my practice, I used ELECT to guide my observations and planning.

For instance, during fine motor activities, I recognized how children were developing coordination and control, and I adjusted my activities to support those skills further. ELECT also helped me understand that each child develops at their own pace, which encouraged me to be flexible and inclusive in my approach. This resource has strengthened my ability to plan intentional learning experiences that support overall development (Ontario Ministry of Education, 2007).

Figure 3. Child Care and Early Years Act (CCEYA) [Cover]. Ontario, 2014.

Child Care and Early Years Act (CCEYA)

I chose the Child Care and Early Years Act (CCEYA) because it provides important guidelines that ensure children's safety, well-being, and quality care in early learning environments. This resource helped me understand my responsibilities as an educator and the importance of maintaining a safe and structured setting for children.

In my practice, I applied CCEYA by being mindful of supervision, maintaining a safe environment, and following proper procedures during daily routines and activities. For example, I ensured that materials used in activities were safe and appropriate for children's age and development. CCEYA has influenced my teaching by helping me recognize that creating a secure and well-organized environment is essential for effective learning and development (Ontario, 2014).

Growth & Reflection

S.M.A.R.T. Goals

Three intentional goals focused on children, families, and colleagues — grounded in my ECE field placement practice.

Continuous Professional Learning

My S.M.A.R.T. Goals

Below are my three S.M.A.R.T. goals developed within the context of my ECE field placements at Humber Polytechnic. Each goal follows the required format: "In order to... so that I... I will... by..."

Goal 1 — Children

Supporting Children's Self-Regulation

In order to support children's self-regulation so that I can help them manage their emotions effectively, I will implement at least two calming and guidance strategies during daily routines by the end of my placement.
Strategy 1: I will introduce calming strategies such as deep breathing, quiet spaces, and visual supports during transitions and moments of frustration. I will observe how children respond to these strategies and adjust them based on individual needs. By consistently using these approaches, I aim to help children recognize their emotions and develop ways to manage them independently. I will also model calm behaviour and guide children through situations where they may feel overwhelmed. This strategy will allow me to create a supportive environment where children feel safe expressing their emotions and gradually build self-regulation skills.
Strategy 2: I will use positive guidance techniques such as redirection, encouragement, and clear communication to support children during challenging situations. Instead of focusing on correcting behaviour, I will guide children toward appropriate actions and help them understand expectations. I will also reflect on my interactions and seek feedback from my mentor to improve my approach. This will help me develop consistency in my responses and strengthen my ability to support children effectively. Over time, this strategy will help children feel understood and supported, which contributes to their emotional development and confidence.
Goal 2 — Families

Strengthening Relationships with Families

In order to strengthen relationships with families so that I can support children's development through collaboration, I will engage in at least one meaningful communication with families each day by the end of my placement.
Strategy 1: I will make an effort to communicate with families during drop-off and pick-up times by sharing observations about their child's day, such as activities they enjoyed or skills they demonstrated. This will help build trust and create a connection between the home and learning environment. I will ensure that my communication is respectful, clear, and supportive. By consistently engaging with families, I aim to create a welcoming environment where families feel comfortable sharing information and asking questions. This will strengthen my ability to work collaboratively and support each child's overall development.
Strategy 2: I will use documentation such as photos, notes, or learning stories to share children's experiences with families. This will allow families to better understand what their children are learning and how they are developing. I will also encourage families to share their insights about their child's interests and needs, which will help me plan more meaningful activities. By using documentation as a communication tool, I will create a stronger connection between the learning environment and the child's home life. This strategy will support family involvement and enhance my ability to create responsive and inclusive learning experiences.
Goal 3 — Colleagues

Improving Professional Collaboration Skills

In order to improve my professional collaboration skills so that I can learn from others and contribute effectively as an educator, I will actively engage in discussions and seek feedback from colleagues throughout my placement.
Strategy 1: I will regularly observe experienced educators and ask questions about their teaching strategies, classroom management, and interactions with children. By doing this, I will gain insight into different approaches and reflect on how I can apply them in my own practice. I will take notes on what I observe and discuss these observations with my mentor to deepen my understanding. This strategy will help me develop confidence and expand my knowledge as an educator while also building strong professional relationships within the team.
Strategy 2: I will actively seek feedback from my mentor and colleagues regarding my planning, interactions, and overall performance. I will use this feedback to reflect on my strengths and identify areas for improvement. By being open to constructive feedback, I will be able to grow professionally and improve my teaching practices. I will also participate in team discussions and contribute my ideas when appropriate. This strategy will help me become more confident in collaborating with others and strengthen my ability to work as part of a professional team.
